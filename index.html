Exploring the underrepresentation of women in tech

Women have constantly been living in the shadows of men throughout history, therefore being excluded from recognition due to society’s natural inclination towards prioritizing men. My Senior Research Project explores the untold stories of these women who have faced many barriers and overcome adversities in order to earn themselves a place in in the Science Technology Engineering and Maths (STEM) industry, with a special focus on women of colour in the tech realm. It incorporates the stories of women who have made great strides in the workplace and have shown aptitude in their work. By delving deeper into the stories of these women and learning through their experiences of community stereotypes, biases, campus culture and senses of belonging, the project highlights the urgent need to increase the representation of women in these fields.

THE CAREER PIPELINE

In order to understand the reasons behind the gender imbalance that is prevalent within the industry, let's take a look at the tech career pipeline. Sketched out below is a generalized representation of what women experience from primary school all the way to their entry into the workplace.

PRIMARY SCHOOL

At early ages, girls begin to lose interest in Science Technology Engineering Mathematics (STEM) subjects. This is due to gender stereotypes, lack of role models in the field and social expectations. Another reason for this is that there are so few mentors to encourage girls to pursue careers in these fields.

HIGH SCHOOL

Early racial, gender, and socioeconomic disparities in access to STEM and computer science education in highschool limit the opportunities that young girls have to engage meaningfully with technology, develop computational skills, and explore careers in computing. These are caused by a multitude of strucutural barriers stemming from poverty and lack of teaching qualifications.

TERTIARY EDUCATION

Disparities in highschool education along with the social and psychological barriers experienced in post-secondary computing environments impact both interest in pursuing computing majors and computing degree completion rates among women and students of color.

WORKPLACE

In the upper ranks of tech companies women struggle to get a seat at the table. Company culture fails to promote women in leadership & does not prioritise the need for diverse teams therefore women find themselves to be in the minority within these organisations.
